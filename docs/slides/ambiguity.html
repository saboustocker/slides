Origin Ambiguity

What happens if people no longer know if something originates from AI or human authorship?

Author

Sabou Rani Stocker

Published

October 29, 2025

Synthesis of existing Research

  1. Differences between Human and AI origin
  2. Ability to differentiate between human origin and AI origin
  3. Consequences of ambiguity of origin
  4. Legal grounds for disclosure

Differences between Human and AI origin

The difference in pereption of materials with human origin compared to materials with AI origin has been researched on several domains:

  • Trustworthiness: generally speaking, people trust humans more than AI
  • Purchase Intentions: Generally, people are less willing to purchase a product, are less loyal to a product, and are less willing to recommend a product, if they know that AI has been used in connotation with this product in any way
  • Attribution & Responsibility: People think AI is more mechanistic, and therefore attribute less bias and more analytical. They also adapt their behavior in response to it.
Trustworthiness
  • people trust AI less than humans in various contexts:
    • AI is perceived to lack human abilities (Kirk and Givi 2025; Lefkeli, Karataş, and Gürhan-Canli 2024)
      • which leads to perceived human decision-makers in the background, which in turn leads to perceived reduction in privacy and increase in feeling exploitet (Lefkeli, Karataş, and Gürhan-Canli 2024)
    • AI is perceived as less private (Lefkeli, Karataş, and Gürhan-Canli 2024)
    • trust is decreased more if AI use is disclosed (Schilke and Reimann 2025; Kirk and Givi 2025) …
    • … and people trust AI less when disclosing things (Lefkeli, Karataş, and Gürhan-Canli 2024)
  • some of these effects (e.g. algorithm aversion (Bigman 2022)) have been discussed extensively already
  • Pfeuffer et al. (2025) show that disclosing the alteration of images decreases trust in content creators / brands
Note Jonas

This is basically “done” at least from the POV of getting any of this published. Trust as an attitude is a pretty basic and thus relatively uninteresting variable imo. It is just a positively valenced variable that can capture both cognitive and affective dimensions, thus it might work as mediator whenever there is either a positive or negative outcome.

Purchase Intention
  • the use of AI decreases the intention to purchase a product, the product loyalty and the willingness to recommend a product
    • if communication is perceived to be written by AI (Kirk and Givi 2025)
    • if generated imagery is generated by AI (Belanche et al. 2025)
      • especially for hedonic services (Belanche et al. 2025)
    • … but if people trust AI, the effect is mitigated (Jung et al. 2025)
    • … and perceived humanness of AI increases purchase intention (Jung et al. 2025)
Note Jonas

My general suggestion would be to look at something that goes beyond purchase/acceptance. The dominant paradigm over the last years has been this aversion vs. acceptance debate. I think we have considerably more upside, if we focus on a more up- or downstream variable along consumers’ journeys with AI (which can be a variable with impact on business or consumers’ self concept etc.). That means, we think about something that is prior to acceptance or not directly relevant to it or we look at usage/interaction etc.

Attribution, Responsibility & Behavior

Attribution

  • because AI is perceived as lacking human abilities (Lefkeli, Karataş, and Gürhan-Canli 2024; Bigman 2022), responsibility is shifted to “humans in the background”
  • This leads to perceiving AI as less biased …(Feldkamp et al. 2024; Bigman 2022; Bonezzi and Ostinelli 2021; Bedemariam and Wessel 2023)
  • … and to less moral outcry when AI makes “biased” decisions (Feldkamp et al. 2024; Bigman 2022; Bonezzi and Ostinelli 2021; Bedemariam and Wessel 2023)

Behavior

  • People display themselves as more analytical when they believe they are assessed by AI instead of humans (Goergen, De Bellis, and Klesse 2025)
  • … (more phenomena in that direction to be researched)

As all of these dimensions have become rather saturated in research over the last couple of years, we will not be focusing our own research on these domains, but use them as a foundation of research already demonstrating various aspects that are different when it comes to Humans vs. AI.

Ability to Differenciate between Human and AI origin

Humans are generally not good at differenciating between AI and human origin. This research goes back several years. As much of the AI generated materials only gets better and less distinguishable from human origin, it is to be assumed that these effects will become even more pronounced in the future.

Primary Sources on Ability to Differenciate
  • “almost 50% of respondents can distinguish between AI and human generated artwork.” (Vukojičić, Krstić, and Veinović 2023)
  • “participants performed below chance levels in identifying AI-generated poems (46.6% accuracy)” (Porter and Machery 2024) “participants were more likely to judge AI-generated poems as human-authored than actual human-authored poems (Porter and Machery 2024)
  • non-artists have pronounced difficulty identifying AI generated images (Ha et al. 2024), but artists and professional artists had much less difficulty (Ha et al. 2024)
  • However, You et al. (2024) did not find a difference between creators and non-creators in accuracy of identifying images
  • AI generated image detectors (e.g. Hive) had no difficulty detecting AI generated images (Ha et al. 2024)
  • With training, participants were able to correctly identify AI generated text correctyl 65% of the time; without training 55% - i.e. slightly above chance (Milička et al. 2025)

Legal Grounds on Disclosure of the Use of AI

Legally, there are few laws regulating the mandated disclosure of AI when generating materials. While there are laws, regulations and acts in relation to the use of AI, they are not necessarily applicable to marketing material for several reasons (Vischer, 2024).

When it comes to assessment or data manipulation using AI, there are strict provisions – at least in the EU (GDPR), Switzerland (FADP), and some other nations (e.g. UK, Australia). While they were not written with the use of AI tools as their primary scope, they apply as soon as personal data is obtained from a consumer, and mandate the disclosure of the use and purpose of this data, as well as any third parties this data is shared with (such as OpenAI when using ChatGPT.)

Many companies increasingly utilize AI when generating marketing and informational materials due to its cost- and time efficiency. At the same time, research indicates that consumers generally trust companies less when the use of AI is disclosed. Therefore, it is to be assumed that there is an incentive to not diclose the use of AI on the companies side.

Regulations pertaining to Personal Data
  • GDPR / FADP as part of the duty to inform people when personal data is obtained to be used with any sort of AI system (GDPR Art. 13/14, FADP Art. 19ff)
  • and for which purposes (GDPR Art. 5(1) and FADP Art. 6(2-3))
  • as well as in the context of consent (GDPR Art. 4(11) & FADP Art. 6(6))
Regulations pertaining to Competition
  • Swiss Labour Act (Ordinance 3, Art. 26) prohibits behavioral monitoring in the workplace unless required. In that case they must be disclosed (Ordinance 3, Art. 6)
  • Federal Act on Cartels and other Restraints of Competition and the Federal Act on Unfair Competition(CH) provides some regulations on transparency, copyright of competitors, and targeting (CartA,UCA)
The EU AI Act

Providers shall ensure that AI systems intended to interact directly with natural persons are designed and developed in such a way that the natural persons concerned are informed that they are interacting with an AI system, unless this is obvious from the point of view of a natural person who is reasonably well-informed, observant and circumspect, taking into account the circumstances and the context of use. This obligation shall not apply to AI systems authorised by law to detect, prevent, investigate or prosecute criminal offences, subject to appropriate safeguards for the rights and freedoms of third parties, unless those systems are available for the public to report a criminal offence.

Providers of AI systems, including general-purpose AI systems, generating synthetic audio, image, video or text content, shall ensure that the outputs of the AI system are marked in a machine-readable format and detectable as artificially generated or manipulated.

Deployers of an AI system that generates or manipulates image, audio or video content constituting a deep fake, shall disclose that the content has been artificially generated or manipulated. This obligation shall not apply where the use is authorised by law to detect, prevent, investigate or prosecute criminal offence.

Where the content forms part of an evidently artistic, creative, satirical, fictional or analogous work or programme, the transparency obligations set out in this paragraph are limited to disclosure of the existence of such generated or manipulated content in an appropriate manner that does not hamper the display or enjoyment of the work.

The information referred to in paragraphs 1 to 4 shall be provided to the natural persons concerned in a clear and distinguishable manner at the latest at the time of the first interaction or exposure.

AI Act, Art. 50

Additional Laws specifically pertaining to AI
  • The United States have several state-wide bills which regulate some uses of AI (e.g. AI transparency Act in California or the Colorado AI Act).

Summary

  • People prefer human over AI
  • Organizations and Companies tend to prefer AI over human due to efficiency and cost*
  • People are bad at identifying AI generated content
  • The law does not provide clear regulations on the (mandated) disclosure of AI, and research shows that AI disclosure decreases trust and purchase intention \(\rightarrow\) motivation for companies to not disclose the use of AI

*although it must be noted that many of these AI applications are still works in progress as well, and this sentiment could very well shift

Therefore, we should assume that companies try to incorporate AI without disclosing it to keep their reputation, whereas consumers prefer human-origin content. Giving companies the benefit of the doubt, we should assume that this conflict happens in situations where law does not prescribe unambiguous behavior (i.e. in information, marketing and branding; but not in assessment or evaluation).

Ambiguity

Definitions

Def. Ambiguity: “Ambiguity is the type of meaning in which a phrase, statement, or resolution is not explicitly defined, making for several interpretations; others describe it as a concept or statement that has no real reference. A common aspect of ambiguity is uncertainty. It is thus an attribute of any idea or statement whose intended meaning cannot be definitively resolved, according to a rule or process with a finite number of steps.” Wikipedia

Def. Origin Ambiguity: Origin ambiguity occurrs when the origin of something generated (text, speech, video, image, or otherwise) cannot be definively resolved*. (current working definition, definitely subject to change!)

*The term origin ambiguity was chosen purposefully over authorship ambiguity, as current law in copyright does not intend for any AI tool to be able to author and therefore own content.

Ambiguity Aversion

People are generally averse to ambiguity. However, while these effects replicate outside of the lab to some extent, they are not as clear-cut as one would think, especially in situations where the risky situation has low winning probabilities or the situaiton pertains to losses. Furthermore, studies on external validity of the effect reveal mixed results.

Primary Sources on Ambiguity Aversion
  • introduced with the Ellsberg paradox in 1961
  • in principle, people prefer the less ambiguous (read: uncertain) bet when provided with several options despite the choice being irrational (Machina and Siniscalchi 2014)
  • The concept of “ambiguity aversion” is not unanimously defined (Machina and Siniscalchi 2014), however in “traditional literature”, it most often means a preference for known risks over unknown risks(Trautmann and Van De Kuilen 2015)
    • ambiguity aversion is not universal: when introducing losses or lower likelihoods, ambiguity aversion is neutralized or even sought after (Kocher, Lahno, and Trautmann 2018) \(\rightarrow\) see also: Kocher (2014), Binmore, stewart & Voorhoeve (2012), Kahn & Sarin (1997)
    • much of this literature has focused on gain-frames (i.e. winning something)
    • however, when the risky urn is a high risk urn, people seem to prefer the ambiguous urn (Curly & Yates, cited in Trautmann and Van De Kuilen (2015))
    • “A significant number of studies found a complete reversal of ambiguity attitudes between the (modest‐likelihood) gain and loss domain, with ambiguity seeking for losses (Abdellaoui, Vossmann, & Weber, 2005; Baillon & Bleichrodt, 2012; Casey & Scholz, 1991; Chakravarty & Roy, 2009; Ho, Keller, & Keltyca, 2002; Kothiyal, Spinu, & Wakker, 2012).” (Trautmann and Van De Kuilen, 2015, p. 105) (pdf)
  • there are also studies on natural ambiguity (e.g. an American prefers to bet on the Dow Jones index, Trautmann and Van De Kuilen (2015))
    • In many of these events, it is shown that people feel more knowledgeable about the event(s) they are familiar with, and also assign higher subjective probabilities to them
  • ambiguity aversion is contested to be a higher-order effect in itself \(\rightarrow\) ambiguity prudence and ambiguity temperance suggested as higher order effects (Baillon, Schlesinger, and Van De Kuilen 2018)
  • ambiguity aversion is also subclassified into absolute and relative ambiguity aversion (Baillon and Placido 2019)
  • many potential moderators are suggested for ambiguity attitudes:
    • comparative ignorance
    • peer effects
    • group decisions
    • market interaction
    • many more (Trautmann and Van De Kuilen 2015)

Ambiguity in Marketing

Ambiguity is a topic that has been discussed in the marketing context extensively and from different lenses. Ambiguity aversion (for general details see above) has shown to correlate to preference for established products (Muthukrishnan, Wathieu, and Xu 2009), and as a moderator to consumer satisfaction [Yi (1993);Erdem and Keane (1996); and others]. Ambiguity is also discussed as a purposeful marketing tactic (Kovacheva and Nikolova 2024).

Ambiguity Aversion and Preference for Established Products
  • ambiguity aversion correlates with preference for more established / well known brands (Muthukrishnan, Wathieu, and Xu 2009)
  • induced ambiguity aversion also predicts preference for established products later on (Muthukrishnan, Wathieu, and Xu 2009)
  • established brands sell stronger in situations where information is presented more ambiguously (Muthukrishnan, Wathieu, and Xu 2009)
Ambiguity as a Moderator of Consumer Satisfaction
  • ambiguity in product quality (Yi 1993; Erdem and Keane 1996):
    • product satisfaction = comparing expectations vs. reality \(\rightarrow\) confirmation / disconfirmation paradigm (Yi 1993)
    • ambiguous product experience when the quality of the product is difficult to evaluate (Yi 1993)
    • consumers evaluate products based on a top-down (expectation and theorizing-driven) approach, when the quality is difficult to evaluate (i.e. ambiguous), but follow a data-driven bottom-up approach when the quality is unambiguous (and neglect expectations) (Yi 1993)
    • ambiguity about product quality is countered by Bayesian learning through the customer (buying different products and updating quality beliefs about \(t-n\) products). Structural modelling of this relationship is better than the Guadagni-Little model, especially the dynamic component appears valuable (Erdem and Keane 1996)
    • when product quality is ambiguous, consumers rely more on advertising to inform themselves (Hoch and Ha 1986)
      • this effect is stronger when the ad is shown before showing the product compared to after, implying that there may be some sort of selective retreival of preceding information (Hoch and Ha 1986)
  • ambiguous marketing offers: ambiguous marketing offers are more effective under scarcity (Dhar et al., 1999), both resource scarcity and availability scarcity (Dhar et al., 1999; Fan et al., 2019)
  • it also depends on how information is displayed: numeric cognition influences how ambiguity aversion manifests (Lenkovskaya and Sweldens 2025) (compression effect & left digit effect)
Role Ambiguity

Def. Role Ambiguity: occurs when a person feels that he or she lacks salient information needed to effectively enact his or her role Singh (1993), p. 12)

  • highly discussed construct in marketing & management [Singh (1993); Ho et al. (1997); and others]

\(\rightarrow\) not highly relevant to our project

Ambiguous (uncertain) Marketing Tactics
  • surprise boxes, buy and reveal deals, random price promotion, teaser ads
  • some classification frameworks, e.g. Kovacheva and Nikolova (2024):
    • assortments: e.g. “loot boxes”
    • promotional: e.g. “incomplete ads”
    • innovation: e.g. “mystery flavor coke”
  • uncertainty marketing is used to cue consumers’ attention and interest (Kovacheva and Nikolova 2024)
  • important for uncertainty marketing: the concealed information is non-negative, i.e. supposed to piqué someone’s interest, not worry them about the “catch”
  • the success of uncertainty marketing depends on many intra-personal characteristics
    • stake: low vs. high
    • opacity: low vs. high \(\rightarrow\) stakes and opacity seem to be independent (Kovacheva and Nikolova 2024)
    • the concept of “lower stakes” is also discussed in other literature: Arrow (1965) for monetary stakes, Duke et al. (2018) in conjunction with uncertainty (uncertainty is less tolerable in high-stakes situations)
    Note Sabou

    While this is not necessarily what we are after, I think the insights regarding stakes could be interesting independent variables for us, i.e. does it matter how high-stakes something is for people to behave differently?

“Origin Ambiguity”

While ambiguity in general has been discussed in various aspects (see sections above), origin ambiguity in terms of not knowing the source of a piece of information has hardly been discussed. There are somewhat related streams of research when looking at what purposefully obfuscating parts of informational material in advertising; when looking at deepfakes (although there is very little research on how people act when they believe something is a deepfake, only how they act when they know it), and (probably most relevant) the uncanny valley effect.

(Origin) Ambiguity due to obfuscated Materials
  • Studies analyzing how cropped photos, etc. ellicit ambiguity
  • Peracchio and Meyers-Levy (1994): visual ambiguity is caused by cropped photos
    • when viewers lack sufficient motivation to parse cropped object, the product evaluations are based on some easily available heuristic (e.g. what the brand claims: “high quality jeans!”) (Peracchio and Meyers-Levy 1994)
      • Johar (1995) finds similar effects for how the information of an ad is perceived under low- vs. high involvement
    • it doesn’t matter if the important object itself is cropped, or if something else is cropped, the “cropping effect” (i.e. the ambiguity) is what makes people involved in processing given enough motivation (Peracchio and Meyers-Levy 1994)
  • Hagtvedt (2011) finds that incomplete typefaces (where letters are obfuscated or left out) ellicit lower trustworthiness, but higher perceived innovativeness
    • trustworthiness is related to clarity, whereas innovativeness is related to interestingness (\(\rightarrow\) this could be something to explore for AI ads, although this effect here relates to letters of logos!)
  • Sevilla and Meyer (2020) find that concealing aspects of an aesthetic product in particular can heighten curiosity and ellicit positive associations
Deepfakes
  • deepfakes are generally hard to detect for humans (see primarily Diel et al. (2024) for a meta-analysis showing that humans are practically as good as chance)
  • there is very little research on the consequences of ambiguity introduced through deepfakes, however:
    • Köbis, Doležalová, and Soraperra (2021) find that people think a video is genuine rather than a Deepfake more often, and they are overconfident in their decisions
    • Whittaker et al. (2025) show that perception of AI alteration (with deepfakes) decreases intention to purchase, and humanness of deepfake increases perceived authenticity (there is however no ambiguity in this study, as people knew what they were looking at)
    • Ahmed, Ng, and Bee (2023) look at the reasons why people share deepfake videos and identify FOMO, low cognitive ability, thinking that they are accurate (duh…)
    • a different study (Soto-Sanfiel, Angulo-Brunet, and Saha 2024) show that most people who do share deepfakes don’t do so for informational purposes, but for hedonic and entertainment
The uncanny valley Effect
  • The uncanny valley hypothesis predicts a negative emotional appraisal of human replicas that appear or behave not quite human

  • the uncanny valley effect is perceived as highly unpleasant (Seyama and Nagayama 2007)
  • systematic literature reviews and meta-analyses show moderate support in favor of the following relevant effects:
    • eeriness in movement causes more distress than eeriness in still images (ka?, $?tsyriReviewEmpiricalEvidence2015)
    • “categorization ambiguity” causes more distress (ka?, $?tsyriReviewEmpiricalEvidence2015)
    • other studies summarize the ambiguity as “categorical uncertainty hypothesis” (Rau 2020). While there is substantial support in favor of these hypotheses, the uncanny valley effect and its causal mechanisms is still somewhat contested (see for example: (MacDorman and Chattopadhyay 2016))
  • there are attempts to quantify the predecessants and consequences of the uncanny valley effect, e.g. with a Bayesian framework (Moore 2012)
  • when we are confronted with the eeriness of the uncanny valley, the amount of information processed is suppressed (Shin, Kim, and Biocca 2019); however this study looks more at the evaluation of the avatars, so maybe not 100% relevant to AI videos
Other Related Work

While there is very little (maybe even no?) research on origin ambiguity in marketing, there are a few constructs somewhat relating to it, namely:

  • authenticity: Silver, Newman, and Small (2021) show that perceived inauthenticity increases outrage at brands. One core driver of perceived inauthenticity is deception, another one is adulteration (i.e. “enhancing”)
  • obfuscation of information: only providing partial information on a product can go both ways. Research shows that products profiting most are those that have the least to hide (see box on obfuscation above)
